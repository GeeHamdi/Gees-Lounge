The Ghost in the Code

Why generative AI can sound intelligent, persuasive, and even thoughtful without possessing human understanding, consciousness, or lived experience.

By Gee Hamdi 12-minute read Artificial Intelligence

If something speaks fluently enough, we instinctively assume there is someone speaking behind the words. That assumption has served humanity remarkably well for thousands of years. Every conversation we have ever had has been with another conscious mind. Every sentence implied an intention, every answer suggested understanding, and every story emerged from lived experience. Language was never merely sound or symbols; it was evidence that another person was present.

Then came large language models.

For the first time in history, millions of people now hold conversations with systems that produce remarkably coherent language without possessing experiences, beliefs, memories in the human sense, or any awareness that a conversation is taking place. This is not simply a technological achievement. It is also a psychological event, because it forces us to confront an uncomfortable possibility: perhaps human beings are far too willing to mistake fluent language for genuine thought.

The more time people spend with systems such as ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, or Copilot, the easier it becomes to forget what these systems actually are. We begin asking questions such as, “Why did the AI decide that?” or “It understands what I mean.” The language itself quietly changes. Before long, we are describing statistical prediction as intention and pattern matching as reasoning. We stop interacting with software and begin interacting with an imagined mind.

This tendency is not new. Human beings apologise to tables after walking into them, shout at computers that refuse to cooperate, and occasionally thank navigation systems for finding a better route. We are natural anthropomorphisers. Reeves and Nass (1996) demonstrated that people routinely apply social rules to computers even while knowing perfectly well that no person is inside the machine.

Large language models intensify this tendency because they do not merely respond. They converse. They apologise, reassure, joke, mirror tone, and adapt their language so convincingly that the boundary between simulation and understanding begins to blur.

Fluency is persuasive because, throughout human history, fluent language has almost always indicated the presence of a mind.

None of this diminishes what these systems accomplish. Their achievements are extraordinary. They summarise complex documents, assist programmers, generate examples, explain difficult concepts, produce drafts, and often communicate with remarkable clarity. Yet extraordinary performance should not tempt us into careless descriptions. The ability to generate convincing language is not identical to understanding that language.

Imagine asking someone to arrange millions of coloured tiles into the shape of a sunset. The finished mosaic may be breathtaking, yet the tiles themselves have no idea what a sunset is. In a similar way, contemporary language models organise relationships among linguistic patterns with astonishing sophistication, but they do not experience grief, curiosity, embarrassment, nostalgia, hunger, pain, or wonder.

They know that the word fire frequently appears near words such as heat, smoke, and burn, but they have never felt warmth. They can describe heartbreak in beautiful prose while remaining entirely untouched by loss. They can discuss fear without ever being afraid.

The distinction matters because meaning, for human beings, is not detached from experience. Words are connected to bodies, memories, places, relationships, sensations, histories, and consequences. The word home is not merely a linguistic token. It may refer to a building, a person, a smell, a loss, a childhood, or somewhere one never wishes to return.

John Searle's (1980) Chinese Room thought experiment remains surprisingly relevant. Imagine a person locked inside a room who follows an enormous rulebook for manipulating Chinese symbols. People outside the room submit questions in Chinese and receive perfectly appropriate answers. To them, it appears that the person inside understands the language. Yet the person may understand nothing at all. They are following rules for arranging symbols.

Whether one accepts every aspect of Searle's argument is less important here than the distinction it reveals: producing meaningful-looking responses is not automatically the same as possessing semantic understanding. Modern language models have made that old philosophical question feel considerably less theoretical.

Perhaps nowhere is the distinction more visible than in AI hallucinations. The word hallucination is itself somewhat misleading because it suggests perception. A more accurate phrase may be confident fabrication.

Ask a language model for a citation, and it may occasionally produce the name of a plausible journal, a convincing title, a realistic volume number, and an article that has never existed. Ask it about a court case, and it may fabricate quotations in perfect legal prose. Ask for a historical detail, and it may provide a polished answer that feels true because every sentence is shaped like the kind of sentence truth usually arrives in.

These errors are revealing. A person who understands the world usually distinguishes between memory, uncertainty, assumption, and invention, though humans are hardly flawless in this respect. A language model predicts what sequence of words is likely to follow a prompt. Accuracy is often the result, but accuracy is not the underlying mechanism.

This is why I find the phrase artificial intelligence both useful and misleading. It captures the remarkable capabilities of these systems, yet it also encourages people to imagine forms of cognition that may not be present. Intelligence is not a single quality. Humans perceive, reason, remember, doubt, imagine, feel, revise beliefs, and interact continuously with a physical and social world.

Current language models are exceptionally capable linguistic systems. That description is already impressive. We do not need to add consciousness where no evidence of consciousness exists, or use the language of intention simply because the output feels intentional.

Ironically, this misunderstanding reveals something about humans as much as it does about machines. We have always used language as evidence of mind because, until recently, that assumption was almost always correct. Our brains developed in a world where coherent words reliably pointed toward conscious speakers.

Large language models challenge that shortcut. The illusion is convincing not because the machine is secretly pretending to be human, but because human beings are deeply responsive to language that behaves as though a speaker exists behind it.

None of this means these technologies should be feared, rejected, or dismissed. I use them myself. They can be powerful collaborators in research, teaching, brainstorming, editing, planning, and learning. The danger lies in allowing fluency to replace critical thinking.

A polished answer still requires verification. A confident explanation still requires evidence. A moving paragraph still requires judgment. The smoother the language becomes, the more important this responsibility may be, because style can disguise uncertainty remarkably well.

The real ghost is ours

Perhaps the ghost in the code is not consciousness hiding inside the machine. It is our own tendency to project consciousness where persuasive language exists. The machine has not suddenly become human. We have simply encountered language detached from the kind of mind we have always assumed must produce it.

That does not make generative AI less remarkable. It makes our responsibility clearer. We can value its capabilities, use it creatively, and learn from it without confusing simulation with experience, prediction with comprehension, or linguistic fluency with a conscious self.
