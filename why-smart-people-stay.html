Why Smart People Stay in Toxic Relationships

Intelligence may help us analyse behaviour, recognise manipulation, and identify unhealthy patterns. It does not make us immune to attachment, hope, fear, or the complicated realities of leaving.

By Gee Hamdi 6-minute read Psychology

There is a particular kind of question people love asking after a relationship ends, usually accompanied by equal parts disbelief and hindsight: “How could someone so intelligent stay with a person like that?” The question is understandable, but it rests on an assumption that has always puzzled me—the assumption that intelligence is somehow a vaccine against emotional pain.

It is an attractive belief because it reassures us. If intelligence protects people from manipulation, then perhaps the world is more predictable than it really is. We tell ourselves that educated people should recognise red flags, that psychologists should never date narcissists, that therapists must have flawless relationships, and that anyone capable of analysing a research paper should also be capable of analysing their own emotions with equal objectivity. Reality is considerably less cooperative.

Some of the brightest people I have known have remained in relationships that slowly eroded their confidence, their sense of self, and sometimes even their ability to trust their own judgement. These were not naïve individuals. Many recognised the manipulation long before they left. Some could explain every psychological mechanism involved—gaslighting, intermittent reinforcement, trauma bonding—better than the people discussing them online. Yet knowing the name of a pattern does not automatically give someone the emotional, social, or practical freedom to leave it.

Knowledge alone rarely determines behaviour. If it did, psychologists would never experience anxiety, physicians would never smoke, and linguists would never misunderstand one another. We often know exactly what would be good for us while continuing to do something else entirely. Human beings are inconvenient that way.

Psychology has long recognised that people do not make decisions through reason alone. Daniel Kahneman’s distinction between fast, intuitive thought and slower, analytical reasoning reminds us that decisions emerge from a constant negotiation between feeling and reflection (Kahneman, 2011). Attachment theorists have similarly argued that early emotional experiences shape what feels familiar in adulthood, even when what is familiar is not particularly healthy (Bowlby, 1988).

This helps explain why one person may interpret unpredictability as a warning while another experiences it as passion. Emotional distance may feel unacceptable to someone raised with consistent care, but strangely familiar to someone who learned early that affection had to be earned. Familiarity is not the same as safety, but the nervous system does not always appreciate the distinction.

Hope also plays a larger role than outsiders often recognise. Toxic relationships rarely begin with an announcement. Few people introduce themselves by saying, “Hello, I intend to undermine your confidence gradually and make every disagreement feel like a criminal investigation.” They may begin with warmth, attentiveness, humour, affection, and the powerful feeling of finally being seen.

When the relationship later changes, the person staying is often not trying to preserve the relationship as it currently exists. They are trying to recover the relationship they remember from the beginning. They are waiting for the affectionate version to return, especially because it does return occasionally. A thoughtful apology, a peaceful weekend, or a sudden display of tenderness can make the entire pattern appear temporary.

Many people do not remain because they cannot see the pattern. They remain because the exceptions keep persuading them that the pattern might finally change.

Communication creates another trap. Having spent years teaching communication, I have repeatedly encountered the belief that every interpersonal problem can eventually be solved through better wording. People rewrite messages, rehearse conversations in the shower, soften their tone, become firmer, provide more examples, and search endlessly for the one sentence that will finally make the other person understand.

Consider the partner who says, “I have explained ten times that disappearing for three days without a message makes me anxious.” The eleventh explanation is unlikely to contain the magical verb that changes everything. The difficulty may no longer be one of comprehension. The other person may understand perfectly well and simply not assign the same importance to the distress being caused.

Watzlawick, Beavin, and Jackson (1967) argued that communication is not merely the transfer of information; it also defines and negotiates relationships. A repeated refusal to acknowledge a clearly expressed need communicates something, even when no direct statement is made. Silence, dismissal, delay, mockery, and strategic confusion can all function as messages.

Intelligent people may be particularly vulnerable to one additional problem: they are very good at producing explanations. Every hurtful action can be connected to childhood trauma, professional pressure, insecurity, fear of intimacy, anxiety, or emotional immaturity. Sometimes those explanations are accurate. They may even help us feel compassion.

But an explanation does not cancel an effect. A person may shout because shouting was normal in their childhood. That history matters. It does not make the shouting harmless. A partner may withdraw because intimacy terrifies them. That fear deserves understanding. It does not mean the other person must live indefinitely in emotional starvation.

This is why I have become cautious of the sentence, “They are not really like that.” What does “really” mean in this context? Are they the affectionate person seen during good weeks, or the cruel person who appears during conflict? Relationships are not lived inside isolated moments. They are lived inside patterns.

None of this means leaving is simple. A relationship may involve children, financial dependence, housing, family pressure, religious or cultural expectations, loneliness, social shame, shared history, disability, immigration status, or fear of retaliation. Someone may also be so psychologically exhausted that the task of beginning again feels larger than the pain of remaining.

Outsiders often ask, “Why did she stay?” because the question allows them to examine the person who remained rather than the conditions that made remaining feel necessary. A better question is: “What made leaving so difficult?” That version is less judgmental and considerably more useful.

Intelligence was never the deciding factor

Intelligent people remain in toxic relationships for many of the same reasons anyone does: attachment, hope, fear, habit, practical dependence, loyalty, shame, and the belief that one more honest conversation may finally repair what has repeatedly broken. Intelligence may help someone describe the situation more accurately. It cannot make the emotional consequences disappear.

Perhaps the lesson is not that intelligent people should know better. It is that human relationships are never governed by knowledge alone. Understanding a harmful pattern can be the beginning of change, but it is not the same as having the resources, readiness, and support required to leave it.

References

Bowlby, J. (1988). A secure base: Parent-child attachment and healthy human development. Basic Books.

Kahneman, D. (2011). Thinking, fast and slow. Farrar, Straus and Giroux.
